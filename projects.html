Academic Projects

  • Machine Learning: Support Vector Machine Research

    In the Spring of 2010, I researched the effects that noise has on Support Vector Machines. The idea for this project came from researching Fuzzy Support Vector Machines, about which you can find more in my introductory presentation on the subject. FSVMs do not seem to have great improvements over normal SVMs -- but why? My hypothesis was that SVMs are naturally fairly resistent to outliers, and so it is rather difficult to achieve better results with FSVMs. I tested this hypothesis by looking specifically at the resiliance of SVMs (trained with a variety of different kernels) to noise -- data points with flipped class labels. My results supported this hypothesis, which you can check out in my original paper or in the results-presentation I gave for the Machine Learning seminar led by Melanie Mitchell at Portland State University.

    For those interested in the technologies, I used LIBSVM to train and test Support Vector Machines, I used R to create the artificial data, I used Python for general scipting, and I used Matplotlib to make the graphs. Eventually I plan on posting the python code online, but it needs some revision before it is public-worthy

  • Modern Cryptography

    In the Winter/Spring of 2010, I participated in the Cryptography Reading Group at Portland State University and led by Tom Shrimpton in which I gave presentations on Lossy Trapdoor Functions, developed by Chris Peikert, and on "Practical Chosen Ciphertext Secure Encryption from Factoring," a paper by Dennis Hofheinz and Eike Kiltz.

    Cryptography lies in a fascinating place between pure mathematics and computer science, and moreover has profound practical applications. I was introduced to cryptography in Tom Shrimpton's introductory course in the Fall/Winter of 2009. For those just getting into cryptography, the best resource out there for modern cryptography is the set of class notes written by the giants of cryptography Mihir Bellare and Phillip Rogaway.

  • Complexity Theory and Mathematical Games: Undergraduate Thesis

    I completed an undergraduate thesis at Reed College in complexity theory, called Mathematical Games, based upon the work of Bob Hearn in the Spring of 2009 with my advisor Jamie Pommersheim. I begin my thesis by looking at the theory behind the hardness classes P, NP, PSPACE, and EXPTIME, and show how they are used in Bob Hearn's Constraint Logic, a general model for games. I then apply Constraint Logic to several games, one of which (Grid) was (to my knowledge) unexplored in the literature before this thesis.

  • Go Diagram and Layout Generation

    In the summer of 2009, I typeset two books of Go problems from various sources (mostly books in Japanese). The first is a book of Tesuji, and the second is a book of Life and Death. I used a slightly modified version of Dan Bump's sgf2dg to create Encapsulated Postscript output from SGF files. Then, I used Python (and eps2pdf) to transform EPS files into PDF and to generate reasonable-looking LaTeX code.
